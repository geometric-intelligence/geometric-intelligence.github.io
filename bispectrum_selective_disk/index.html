Modern vision systems learn powerful representations, but often at the cost of huge data and compute to discover invariances that could, in principle, be built in. To what extent should invariance be learned, rather than encoded directly? We propose the selective disk bispectrum: an analytical, rotation-invariant encoder—an explicit map from pixels to coefficients—with inversion, theory under noise, and strong empirical behavior next to today’s large models.

What is the selective disk bispectrum?

Think of it as a rotation-invariant encoder for 2-D images: a fixed feature map that throws away global rotation while keeping the information needed for recognition and recovery. It is an analytical representation—specified by transforms and algebra, not by gradient descent—so it offers invariance by design rather than invariance discovered from data.

The construction builds on the bispectrum, a classical tool built from tensor products of irreducible representations (irreps). Bispectral statistics form a class of complete invariants for the group action: they preserve information about the signal up to the symmetry (here, rotation), and they retain phase-sensitive, higher-order correlations that second-order spectra discard. Our focus is the disk setting—rotations in the plane—related to disk-harmonic and cryo-EM style formulations. The “selective” part means we keep a minimal subset of coefficients still sufficient for reconstruction, instead of the full redundant bispectrum.

This sits between two familiar paradigms. Learned encoders—CNNs and vision transformers, including DINO, CLIP, and EVA—fit parameters to data and can approximate irreps implicitly during training. Analytical descriptors—power spectra, classical bispectra, disk harmonics, Hu moments, SIFT—bake symmetries into the map. Group-equivariant networks bridge the two by putting symmetry into the architecture while still learning weights. The selective disk bispectrum is fully analytical: no learned convolutional trunk is required to obtain the invariant code.

Why does this matter?

State-of-the-art visual models trade scale for expressivity: many invariances and equivariances could be stated a priori, yet are left for the optimizer to find. Work in geometric deep learning emphasizes that building symmetry into representations can improve efficiency and generalization; the design goal is a map that is simultaneously informative, compact, and invariant to nuisance transforms like rotation.

Learned models must implicitly discover invariance when it is not hard-coded—often by approximating irreps across layers—which can inflate sample and compute needs. A complementary route is to construct features directly from irreps, so equivariance and invariance are explicit. The bispectrum is a canonical example: it aggregates global structure through structured algebraic interactions of irreps rather than stacking opaque nonlinear blocks, which can concentrate information in low-dimensional statistics and suggests favorable behavior in low signal-to-noise settings—unlike depth-wise propagation where signal is spread across many layers.

Prior bispectrum-based pipelines still face limits. Some variants (e.g. selective group bispectrum-style setups) lean on learned convolutional layers, which costs compute and can hurt robustness. The full bispectrum also contains many coefficients, a large fraction of which are dominated by noise and obscure the signal. The disk bispectrum from cryo-EM and related literature lacked a tractable, analytical inverse on images—blocking principled coefficient selection and efficient use as a drop-in encoder.

Empirically, on rotation-invariant classification benchmarks, the selective disk bispectrum is competitive with group convolutions, DINO, EVA, and CLIP while being much faster to compute, and remains interpretable because the map is closed-form rather than learned.

How does it work?

We introduce the selective disk bispectrum as an analytical, rotation-invariant feature map on top of the disk bispectrum framework. The paper develops three main technical advances.

First—invertibility and selection. We derive the first analytical inversion of the disk bispectrum. That inverse identifies which coefficient combinations carry the signal and yields a minimal set of coefficients sufficient for reconstruction, turning an unwieldy full bispectrum into a practical encoder.

Second—noise. We analyze the mean and variance of bispectral coefficients under Gaussian noise. The selective rule removes a large share of noise-dominated terms; the resulting estimator stays close to the noiseless signal in expectation with reduced variance relative to retaining everything.

Third—scaling. Under this construction, signal-to-noise ratio scales linearly with the input noise level in the Gaussian setting we analyze—a sharp contrast with the degradation often seen as noise passes through deep learned encoders. Intuitively, the bispectrum pools information through fixed algebraic structure instead of learned depth.

Together, these pieces define a rotation-invariant encoder you can run as a deterministic front end: fixed irrep-based statistics, explicit inversion for analysis and alignment tasks, and a selection principle grounded in both reconstruction and noise.

What did we find?

We validate the story in three directions: classification under controlled noise, multi-reference alignment (following classical bispectrum alignment frameworks), and agreement between theory and practice on robustness and SNR behavior.

  • Analytical encoder. A rotation-invariant feature map from irreps—invariance by design—without learned convolutional layers in the bispectral core.
  • Inversion. First analytical inverse of the disk bispectrum, enabling principled selection of a minimal reconstructing set.
  • Noise theory. Mean and variance of coefficients under Gaussian noise; selection drops noise-dominated terms and improves concentration of the estimate.
  • Benchmarks. Under noise corruption, strong performance relative to convolutional networks and large pretrained models (CLIP, DINO, EVA), e.g. on benchmarks such as MNIST in the paper’s setting—alongside competitive rotation-invariant classification against group convolutions with much faster feature extraction.
  • Multi-reference alignment. Applied to MRA to show predicted noise robustness translates to downstream inference.
  • SNR scaling. Empirical identification of Gaussian-noise SNR scaling linear in the input noise level, consistent with the theory.

Overall, these results support a simple message: building irreducible structure and invariance directly into the encoder can be a powerful alternative to purely data-driven representations—especially when robustness, efficiency, and interpretability matter as much as raw accuracy.
