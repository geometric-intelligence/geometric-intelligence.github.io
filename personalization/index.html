When Machine Learning Gets Personal: Evaluating Prediction and Explanation

Louisa Cornelis1, Guillermo Bernárdez1, Haewon Jeong1, Nina Miolane1
1University of California, Santa Barbara
ICLR 2026
Paper arXiv OpenReview

Abstract

In high-stakes domains like healthcare, users often expect that sharing personal information with machine learning systems will yield tangible benefits, such as more accurate diagnoses and clearer explanations of contributing factors. However, the validity of this assumption remains largely unexplored. We propose a unified framework to quantify how personalizing a model influences both prediction and explanation. We show that its impacts on prediction and explanation can diverge: a model may become more or less explainable even when prediction is unchanged. For practical settings, we study a standard hypothesis test for detecting personalization effects on demographic groups. We derive a finite-sample lower bound on its probability of error as a function of group sizes, number of personal attributes, and desired benefit from personalization. This provides actionable insights, such as which dataset characteristics are necessary to test an effect, or the maximum effect that can be tested given a dataset. We apply our framework to real-world tabular datasets using feature-attribution methods, uncovering scenarios where effects are fundamentally untestable due to the dataset statistics. Our results highlight the need for joint evaluation of prediction and explanation in personalized models and the importance of designing models and datasets with sufficient information for such evaluation.

Paper Summary

What is the problem?

In critical domains like healthcare, machine learning models are increasingly personalized by incorporating attributes that encode personal characteristics, such as sex, race, or age. Users implicitly expect that sharing this personal information will improve predictions—but does personalization consistently deliver? While it can enhance accuracy (e.g., cardiovascular risk models that account for sex), it can also amplify biases and harm specific groups. Crucially, the effect of personalization on explanation quality—how well a model justifies its decisions—has never been formally studied.

What did we do?

We propose a unified framework to quantify how personalizing a model influences both prediction accuracy and explanation quality. We formally prove that the impacts on prediction and explanation can diverge: a model may become more or less explainable even when prediction is unchanged. For practical settings, we derive finite-sample lower bounds on the probability of error for hypothesis tests that detect personalization effects, generalizing prior theory beyond binary classification to regression and explanation metrics. We apply the framework to real-world healthcare datasets including MIMIC-III.

What did we find?

  • Prediction and explanation can diverge: We prove that a personalized model can match a generic model in accuracy yet offer better (or worse) explanations. Focusing only on prediction can overlook significant interpretability gains—or mask harms.
  • Testability depends on task type: In classification, the lower bound on hypothesis test error is identical for prediction and explanation—either both are testable, or neither is. In regression, the bounds differ, so one may be testable while the other is not.
  • Large empirical gains can be misleading: A high observed benefit of personalization does not guarantee a valid conclusion. Even the largest apparent benefit in our experiments (sufficiency in regression) turned out to be untestable, while a much smaller benefit for prediction was statistically reliable.
  • Personalization is hard to evaluate in medical settings: Across healthcare datasets (MIMIC-III, UCI Heart), many tests are fundamentally unreliable due to dataset statistics, pointing to a key limitation of personalized medicine.
